Level 1 · Easiest · 1–2 weeks to learn

Following recipes.

Like a home cook using a recipe book. You didn't invent the dish — you're just following instructions really well.

The grown-up name: prompt engineering. You write very specific instructions for an AI. "You are a friendly support agent for a yoga studio. Answer in 3 sentences max. Always ask one follow-up question." That's it.

Level 2 · Still pretty easy · 1–2 months to learn

Cooking with the cookbook open.

Like a student taking an open-book exam. They don't memorize everything — they look up the answer when they need it.

The grown-up name: RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation). The AI reads your company's documents in real time and uses them to answer questions. Build this once, use it forever. This is what 80% of "AI companies" actually do.

Level 3 · Getting real · 2–4 months to learn

Training your own chef.

Take a smart generalist and send them to a 3-month course on your specific business. They go from "smart in general" to "expert in your thing."

The grown-up name: fine-tuning. You take an open model like Llama or Qwen and teach it your style, your tone, your domain. You can do this on a single rented GPU for a few hundred dollars.

Level 4 · The big leagues · years, $1M+

Inventing a new cuisine.

Not cooking a dish — designing an entirely new way of cooking that nobody's seen before.

The grown-up name: training a model from scratch. This is what DeepSeek, OpenAI, Anthropic do. It costs millions to billions of dollars, takes a research team, and requires GPU clusters. Skip this unless it's literally your business.

Where to actually start: Level 1 this week. Level 2 next month. Level 3 only if Levels 1 and 2 don't solve your problem. Level 4 only if you're starting a frontier AI lab (which, by the way, is harder than starting a rocket company — both in money and in talent).
