The big idea: A neural network is a chain of tiny math helpers. Each one takes the food and changes it a tiny bit. By the end, the food has become the answer.

ingredients → chop → mix → bake → cake. That's a neural network.

Now, how do the chefs learn?

Analogy · The taste test

Imagine teaching a kid to make lemonade.

The kid makes a glass. You taste it. "Too sour." The kid adds a little sugar. You taste again. "Better, but too sweet now." The kid adjusts. After 1,000 tries, the kid makes perfect lemonade every time.

That's how neural networks learn. They make a prediction. We check how wrong they were. They adjust. They try again. A million times. After enough tries, they get really, really good.

The grown-up name: this is called backpropagation. The fancy word just means "tell each chef how much they messed up, so they can do better next time."

Analogy · LEGO pieces

Models don't read words. They eat LEGO.

A model chops text into smaller pieces called tokens. "Hello world" might become two pieces. "Cryptocurrency" might become four pieces. The model thinks in pieces, not whole words.

Why care? Every model has a context window — how many pieces it can hold in its head at once. Bigger window = smarter answers, but slower and more expensive.
