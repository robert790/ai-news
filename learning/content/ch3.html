Story · The hedge fund that pivoted

A math wizard who loves puzzles.

In Hangzhou, China, a quiet math lover named Liang Wenfeng built one of the country's top trading hedge funds called High-Flyer. The fund's edge? Using AI to predict stock prices. To do that, they built a massive GPU cluster — 50,000 powerful chips.

One day, Liang looked at the chips and thought: what if we used these not to predict stocks, but to build a brain? So he spun off a new company: DeepSeek.

The world didn't notice at first. Then, in January 2025, they released a model called R1 that was as smart as the best American models — and they gave it away for free. Stock markets panicked. OpenAI looked worried. The AI world shifted.

Their three kitchen tricks.

Trick 1 · Call only the chefs you need

Mixture of Experts (MoE).

Imagine a kitchen with 60 chefs. But every dish only needs 6 of them. The other 54 sit and drink tea. That's MoE: instead of using the whole model, you only wake up the parts you need.

Why it matters: DeepSeek's biggest model has 671 billion parameters (a "parameter" is one tiny dial on a chef). But it only uses 37 billion at a time. That's why their model is so fast and so cheap.

Trick 2 · Tiny notebooks

Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA).

When you read a book, you don't memorize every page. You scribble a tiny note. MLA is the same idea — instead of holding the whole conversation in memory, the model keeps a tiny summary. Same answers, way less memory, way faster.

Why it matters: serving an AI model is expensive because of memory. MLA cuts that cost dramatically. That's why DeepSeek can charge 10× less than OpenAI for similar quality.

Trick 3 · Learning by tasting your own food

Pure reinforcement learning (the R1 trick).

Normally, you teach a model by giving it lots of examples: "here's the right answer, copy it." DeepSeek proved you don't need that. You can just tell the model "good answer" or "bad answer," and it figures out how to get more good answers on its own.

Why it matters: this was a huge deal. It means models can learn to reason — to think step by step — without humans writing the steps for them. It's the discovery that started the whole "reasoning model" trend in 2025.

The real lesson from DeepSeek: they didn't beat OpenAI by having more chips. They beat them by being smarter with the chips they had. That's the lever you have access to too. Not size. Cleverness.
