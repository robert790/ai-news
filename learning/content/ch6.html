The big idea: read like a journalist, not a referee. You're trying to extract "what did they do and why should I care?" — not verify every equation. 80% of the paper is for people who actually need to reproduce it. You don't.

Anatomy of an AI paper.

1

Abstract

One paragraph. What they did, what they found, why it matters. Read this first.

read · 2 min
2

Introduction

The "why this matters" setup. Usually has a great diagram showing the new method. Skim the diagrams.

skim · 5 min
3

Related Work

"Here's what other people did before us." Boring for outsiders. Skip unless you need context.

skip
4

Method / Approach

The actual how-it-works. Full of math. Read the first paragraph and any diagrams, skip the equations.

skim only
5

Experiments

"Here's how it performed." Look at the tables and the bold numbers. Read the conclusions from the tables.

tables · 5 min
6

Conclusion / Discussion

What the authors think is important. Often mentions limitations honestly. Read this.

read · 3 min

Total time for a real paper: 15–20 minutes. You don't need to understand everything. You need to understand the point.

Try it: DeepSeek-V3, page 1.

The DeepSeek-V3 paper (the one that broke the internet) opens like this — translated from "academic English" into "actually-English":

Translation · DeepSeek-V3, plain English

"We trained a 671B-parameter model for $5.5M and it works really well."

What they actually did: built a model with 671 billion parameters (the dials). Used a clever architecture (MoE) so only 37 billion are active per question. Trained it on 14.8 trillion tokens (LEGO pieces) of text. The whole final training run cost about $5.5 million in compute. It scores as well as the best closed models on math, code, and reasoning benchmarks.

Why it matters: it proves that the frontier isn't gated by trillion-dollar budgets. Smart design can substitute for raw scale.

What to ignore: the 53-page Methods section. You don't need to reproduce it.
