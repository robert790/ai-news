Story · The shovel that grew

A 19-year-old with a treasure map.

Picture a teenager who draws a map and says: "There's gold buried over there." Everyone in the village laughs. They keep laughing for years. The teenager keeps digging — quietly, with a small shovel. One day, after many years, the gold starts showing. Suddenly the same people who laughed pull out their checkbooks.

That teenager's name is Sam Altman. The treasure is Artificial General Intelligence — a computer as smart as a human.

  • 1985

    Born in Chicago, grew up in St. Louis.

    Smart kid, but not a prodigy. Computer-obsessed from age 8.

  • 2005

    Drops out of Stanford. Builds his first app, Loopt.

    Sells it for $43M a few years later. He's 26.

  • 2014

    Becomes president of Y Combinator.

    The world's most famous startup accelerator. He sees thousands of founders a year. He learns to spot winners.

  • 2015

    Co-founds OpenAI with Elon Musk and others.

    Promise: build AI safely, for everyone. Initial budget: a billion dollars from donors.

  • 2019

    Convinces Microsoft to invest $1 billion.

    Most people thought this was crazy. Microsoft trusted him.

  • 2022

    ChatGPT launches. 100 million users in 2 months.

    The fastest-growing app in human history. The whole world wakes up.

  • 2023

    Microsoft invests $10 billion more. He's fired by his own board. Five days later, he's rehired.

    Almost every employee threatened to quit. Investors threatened to pull money. The board blinked.

  • 2025

    OpenAI valued at ~$300 billion. Net worth: ~$2 billion.

    Not from salary. From owning a small slice of the company. Same playbook as every tech founder who wins big.

What Sam teaches us: he's not a researcher. He can't write the math that builds a neural network. His superpower is something else entirely — he refuses to give up on a vision that looks impossible to everyone else, and he gets other powerful people to believe it too.

You probably can't be Sam. You probably don't have his network, his dealmaking, or his nerve. But the lesson is portable: pick a direction that looks crazy to other people, then refuse to flinch.
